# Role-specific interview prep frameworks

Use these as scaffolding only. Every framework must be grounded in *this specific company's* JD language, hiring manager statements, or real candidate reports — generic prep advice exists everywhere; the value here is the connection to the target company.

## The general loop pattern (most modern tech companies)

1. **Recruiter screen** — values, comp expectations, basic background
2. **Hiring manager call** — depth on background, fit for the team
3. **Role-specific exercise** — varies by role (see below)
4. **Cross-functional panels** — typically 30–60 min each with future peers
5. **Exec or skip-level chat** — often a final-round signal check

Variations: some companies front-load the exercise (homework before hiring manager); some run on-sites in a single day; some skip the exec round for IC roles.

## Past-project / portfolio presentation

Used at design-conscious or craft-focused companies. Common for PM, Design, sometimes ML/Research.

**What it tests:** storytelling, judgment in hindsight, the quality of past work. Not live reasoning.

**Picking the project:**
- Lead role (driver, not co-pilot)
- Real ambiguity at the start
- Visible tradeoffs (what was chosen NOT to do)
- Measurable outcomes

**Structure to recommend:**
1. Context & problem (who was the user, why this mattered)
2. Discovery (how they figured out it was worth solving)
3. Options considered (show the decision tree, not just the chosen path)
4. What they shipped (visuals, not walls of text)
5. Impact (metrics, qualitative outcomes)
6. What they'd do differently (anticipated question; needs a real answer, not a humble-brag)

**Likely probes:**
- Why did you pick this project?
- How did you know this was worth solving?
- What did you decide NOT to do?
- What went wrong?
- What would you do differently?
- Walk through a cross-functional conflict.

**Logistics tips:**
- Prep both a 15-min and 30-min version — the slot may compress
- Slides minimal and visual; heavy text reads poorly at craft-focused companies
- Practice the Q&A more than the slides
- If possible, weave in a reference to the company's actual product (signals genuine interest)

## Behavioral / storytelling rounds

Universal. Most loops include at least one behavioral round.

**Common question categories:**
- Ambiguity / driving without clear direction
- Cross-functional collaboration (good + bad story expected)
- Conflict resolution
- Failure / learning
- Influence without authority

**Story framework:** STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the default. Anchor every story in 90 seconds of setup + 3 minutes of action + 30 seconds of outcome.

**Watch for double questions** — e.g., "tell me about a good AND a bad collaboration" is two stories. Don't drop the second half.

**Trap to avoid:** trashing a former colleague, even gently. Frame failures as systems or process issues the candidate could have shaped, not as another person being bad at their job.

## Product sense case (PM-specific)

Live problem-solving exercise. The interviewer presents a problem; the candidate walks through how they'd approach it.

**Common formats:**
- "Design X for user segment Y"
- "How would you improve [the company's product]?"
- "Choose a product you love; how would you grow it?"

**Framework:**
1. Clarify the problem (always)
2. Identify the user and their job-to-be-done
3. List possible solutions (breadth, not depth)
4. Pick one with explicit tradeoffs
5. Define success metrics
6. Walk through risks / what to test

**Tactical:** the interviewer's signal isn't the answer — it's the structure and the questions the candidate asks first. Better answers ask 2–3 clarifying questions before proposing anything.

## Technical / coding rounds (engineering)

Varies wildly by company. Common formats:

- LeetCode-style algorithmic problems (less common now at startups)
- Take-home project (more common)
- Pair-programming on a realistic small task
- Code review of a candidate's prior work or a provided snippet
- System design (separately, usually a dedicated round)

**For pair-programming:** narrate thinking out loud. Ask before adding dependencies. Write small, working slices first; optimize later.

**For take-homes:** treat README quality as 30% of the grade. Tests matter. A working 70% solution beats an incomplete 100% attempt.

## System design rounds (senior engineering, sometimes senior PM)

Open-ended architecture conversation. Common prompts:

- Design Twitter's timeline / Uber's matching / Slack's messaging
- Design a system that does [specific company-relevant thing]

**Framework:**
1. Clarify scope (read-heavy? write-heavy? geographic distribution? scale?)
2. Define API surface
3. Sketch data model
4. Sketch high-level architecture
5. Identify bottlenecks
6. Discuss tradeoffs

The signal is judgment under uncertainty — not memorizing the "right" architecture.

## Exec / CEO chat

Final-round signal. Tests for fit with leadership and the candidate's reasons for joining.

**Anticipate:**
- Why this company specifically?
- What questions do you have for me?
- What's the most important thing you've learned recently?
- What would make this role a bad fit for you?

**Tactical:** the candidate's questions here matter more than answers. Prep 3–5 questions that show they've thought about the company's strategic position.

## Recruiter screen

Easy to underestimate. Recruiters gatekeep onsite scheduling and often pass the loop their first-impression notes.

**Anticipate:**
- Why are you looking?
- What's your comp expectation?
- What's your timeline?
- Tell me about your background in 2 minutes.

**Tactical:** give a tight, structured 2-min background. Have a comp answer ready that doesn't anchor too low (research the company's bands first via Levels.fyi / Glassdoor / the JD itself).

## When the candidate has the question in advance

If they were sent a specific question before the round, do three things:

1. **Capture it verbatim** in the packet
2. **Cross-reference it against past Glassdoor reports** — companies often reuse questions across roles. A question previously asked of a different role tells you what trait they're testing for in general.
3. **Build a framework + specific story** for it. Frameworks alone read as canned; stories alone read as anecdotal. The combination is strongest.

## Day-before checklist (universal)

- Sign up for and use the company's product, even briefly. Mention it organically in the loop.
- Read the hiring manager's LinkedIn, including recent posts.
- Skim 1–2 recent product launches or engineering blog posts.
- Rehearse out loud, not silently. Time the practice runs.
- Prepare 3–5 questions to ask. Leave room for new ones that emerge during the conversation.
- Sleep, eat, hydrate. The last 5% of polish never beats being clear-headed.
